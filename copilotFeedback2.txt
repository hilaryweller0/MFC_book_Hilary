2. The main structural issue: the chapter is still too encyclopaedic
This is the single biggest remaining issue — and it’s a good problem to have.
The problem
The chapter sometimes slips from:

“Here is the story of how numerical weather prediction evolved”

into:

“Here is a compact review article of 60 years of atmospheric numerics.”

For a review paper, that’s fine.
For a book chapter aimed at incoming PhD students, it can overwhelm.
The consequence
Some readers will:

lose the hierarchy of ideas,
struggle to remember what mattered most,
confuse “important historically” with “important today”.


3. The key conceptual distinction to sharpen
If I had to summarise the chapter’s implicit thesis, it is:

Weather and climate numerics are dominated by constraints that come from fast waves, geometry, and computers — not from the slow physics we care about.

That thesis is present everywhere — but not always foregrounded.
Suggestion: make constraints explicit
When introducing any method, grid, or discretisation, consider explicitly stating:

What constraint it addresses
What new constraint it introduces

For example:


Semi‑implicit methods
→ remove fast‑wave time‑step limits
→ introduce elliptic solves


Latitude–longitude grids
→ simple numerics
→ destroy parallel scalability


Spectral methods
→ maximal accuracy per DOF
→ global communication


Right now, the reader often has to infer this themselves. Making it explicit would greatly strengthen the chapter.

4. Section‑by‑section constructive criticism
4.1 Early sections (Richardson → Charney)
These are very strong, but I’d suggest slightly more signposting for mathematicians.
Example improvement:

“Richardson classified weather prediction as a ‘marching’ problem…”

You could add:

In modern PDE terms, he correctly identified the equations as hyperbolic — but underestimated the numerical consequences of fast characteristic speeds.

That single sentence bridges disciplines beautifully.

4.2 Taming Fast Waves (the conceptual heart)
This is one of the strongest sections, but it could be even sharper.
What works

Excellent choice of shallow‑water example
Clear link between wave speed and CFL
Exercises that force understanding, not memorisation

What could improve
At the end of this section, explicitly state:

Fast waves are not eliminated because they are unphysical, but because they dominate numerical stability while contributing little to forecast skill.

That sentence would crystallise the entire section.

4.3 Arakawa grids
This is technically excellent — but dense.
Suggestion
Add a one‑paragraph executive summary at the start:

Arakawa grids are best understood as different compromises between accurate wave propagation, avoidance of computational modes, and accurate Coriolis coupling.

Then the detailed discussion feels guided rather than exhaustive.

4.4 Spectral method section
This section is very clear mathematically — perhaps too comfortable for maths graduates.
Constructive critique
You might consider:

briefly flagging aliasing and non‑locality as the two main numerical issues,
even if you don’t fully treat them.

That signals awareness of real‑world complications without adding bulk.

4.5 Meshes of the sphere and parallelism
This is one of the best parts of the chapter.
If anything, I’d encourage you to be more opinionated here. For example:

At modern resolutions, latitude–longitude grids are not merely inefficient — they are a fundamental barrier to scalability.

That’s defensible, and students appreciate clarity.

4.6 SEM vs DG
This comparison is good but could be crisper.
Right now it’s descriptive; it could be more decisive.
A suggested contrast sentence:

Spectral elements maximise accuracy for smooth, balanced flows, while discontinuous Galerkin methods prioritise robustness, locality, and scalability in the presence of sharp gradients.

This helps students remember why both exist.

5. Style and pacing: small but important points
✅ What to keep

Occasional informal phrasing (“treacle”, “heroic”)
Historical anecdotes
Explicit references to real models (ECMWF, ICON, CAM)

⚠️ What to trim

Repeated reminders of facts already established (e.g. CFL limits)
Some derivations could be shortened with “details omitted” signposts

Key messages from this chapter
